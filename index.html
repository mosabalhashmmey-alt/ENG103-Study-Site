Overview

This chapter covers the foundations of Technical Writing for ENG 103, including its definitions, differences from academic writing, main features, purposes, and common document types.

Study Beats Content

1. What is technical communication?

Definition: The exchange of information that helps people interact with technology, achieve workplace goals, and solve complex problems.

Examples: Cell phone instruction manuals, printer/microwave oven setup information, online courses, writing emails and reports, etc.

2. Technical Writing vs. Academic Writing
  • Technical Writing: Reader based, task oriented, and design based.
  • Academic Writing: Writer based, supporting the writer's stance, and relies on paragraph form.
3. Who creates technical communication?
  • All professionals function as technical communicators.
  • Anyone in a workplace setting can be a technical communicator.
  • Experts in any field are often required to present their knowledge to nonexpert audiences.
4. Main features of technical communication
  1. Focuses on the reader not the writer (user-centered communication).
  2. Is efficient and accessible.
  3. Is clear and relevant.
  4. Uses media effectively.
  5. Is created by both individuals and teams.
  6. Targets a global audience.
  7. Is persuasive and truthful.
  8. Is based on research.
5. Three purposes of technical communication
  • Informational purpose: Anticipate and answer questions.
  • Instructional purpose: Help people to perform a task.
  • Persuasive purpose: Encourage readers to take an action.
6. Common types of technical documents
  • Memos
  • Emails
  • Letters
  • Instructions
  • Procedures
  • Manuals
  • Brochures
  • Proposals
  • Reports
1. Importance of Proofreading

Basic errors will distract the reader and make the writer look careless.

2. Effective Strategies
  • Save it for the final draft: Don't proofread while drafting.
  • Take a break: Rest before proofreading.
  • Work from hard copy: Spot mistakes on paper.
  • Proofread multiple times: Read more than once.
  • Don't rely only on AI: Never rely completely on Grammarly.
1. Thinking Critically About Research

Critical thinking means testing the quality of information and accuracy of interpretations. Instead of accepting information at face value, you should examine, evaluate, verify, analyze, and weigh alternatives.

Effective Strategies: Ask the right questions, explore a balance of views, explore topic in depth, evaluate sources, and interpret findings objectively.

2. Primary Vs. Secondary Sources
  • Primary Research: Getting information directly from the source (e.g., conducting interviews, surveys, observing people or events).
  • Secondary Research: Getting information second-hand by reading compiled works (e.g., books, articles, websites).
3. Hard-Copy Vs. Web-Based Sources

Hard-Copy Sources:

  • Benefits: Found on shelves with related materials, easy to determine author/date, easier to preserve and keep secure.
  • Drawbacks: Time-consuming to search, offer only text/images, hard to update.

Web-Based Sources:

  • Benefits: More current and accessible, searches can be narrowed/broadened easily, offers material with no hard-copy equivalent.
  • Drawbacks: Might only access recent material, not always reliable, user might get confused by too many choices.
4. Strategies for Internet Research
  • Select technical keywords rather than general ones.
  • Identify the site's purpose and sponsor.
  • Assess the currency of the site and its materials.
  • Assess the author's credentials and assertions.
  • Download only what you need, use it ethically, and cite sources.
5. Categories of Hard-Copy Reference Works
  • Bibliographies
  • Indexes
  • Encyclopedias
  • Dictionaries
  • Handbooks
  • Almanacs
  • Directories
  • Abstracts
  • Gray Literature
